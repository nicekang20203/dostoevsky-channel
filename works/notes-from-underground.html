Notes from Underground

The first existentialist novel. A manifesto against rational utopianism.

The Underground Man

The unnamed narrator is a retired civil servant living in a shabby St. Petersburg apartment. He is bitter, contradictory, and deeply self-aware. He tells us he is sick, spiteful, and unattractive — and then admits he was lying about most of it. We can trust nothing he says, yet we cannot look away.

He is writing, he claims, for himself alone. Yet he addresses an imaginary audience constantly, anticipating their objections, mocking their assumptions. The Underground Man is the first great unreliable narrator in modern literature.

I am a sick man... I am a spiteful man. I am an unattractive man.

— Opening lines

Against the Crystal Palace

The novella is a direct attack on the rational utopianism of Nikolai Chernyshevsky, whose novel What Is to Be Done? imagined a perfect society based on rational self-interest. The Crystal Palace — the glass-and-iron exhibition hall in London — became a symbol of this rationalist paradise.

The Underground Man rejects this vision utterly. He argues that humans will never act according to their rational self-interest — that we will deliberately choose suffering, chaos, even destruction, simply to prove that we are free and not piano keys.

Liza

In Part II, the Underground Man encounters Liza, a young prostitute. In a remarkable scene, he delivers a sermon on her degradation, moving her to tears with his eloquence about the life she could have. She comes to him seeking salvation — and he cruelly humiliates her, unable to accept genuine human connection.

Why It Matters

Notes from Underground is widely considered the first existentialist novel. It anticipates Nietzsche, Freud, and the entire twentieth-century exploration of alienation, bad faith, and the irrational depths of human nature. Sartre, Camus, and Kafka all acknowledged their debt to Dostoevsky's underground man.
