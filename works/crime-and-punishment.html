Crime and Punishment Преступление и наказание

The novel that made Dostoevsky immortal — a psychological journey into guilt, redemption, and the limits of human reason.

The Story

Rodion Romanovich Raskolnikov is a former law student living in a cramped garret in St. Petersburg. Destitute and isolated, he has developed a theory: that extraordinary people — Napoleons — have the right to transgress moral law for the greater good. To prove he belongs to this elite, he murders Alyona Ivanovna, an elderly pawnbroker, with an axe. Her half-sister Lizaveta, an innocent simpleton, stumbles upon the scene; he kills her too.

What follows is not a detective story but a psychological descent. Raskolnikov cannot enjoy his "triumph." He becomes feverish, paranoid, unable to touch the stolen money hidden beneath a rock. The brilliant investigator Porfiry Petrovich suspects him but has no evidence — only psychological pressure, which he applies with surgical precision across three unforgettable interrogation scenes.

Salvation comes through Sonya Marmeladova, a young woman forced into prostitution to support her family. She reads Raskolnikov the story of Lazarus from the Gospel of John — the dead man raised to life. In the novel's climax, Raskolnikov confesses publicly at the Haymarket Square, then surrenders to the police. He is sentenced to eight years in Siberia. Sonya follows him. In the epilogue, he finally accepts her love and, with it, the possibility of resurrection.

"Pain and suffering are always inevitable for a large intelligence and a deep heart. The really great men must, I think, have great sadness on earth."
— Part III, Chapter 5

Historical Context

Dostoevsky wrote Crime and Punishment in desperate circumstances. He was deeply in debt, his journal Epoch had failed after his brother Mikhail's death in 1864, and he had signed a predatory contract with the publisher Stellovsky. He wrote much of the novel while serializing it in The Russian Messenger, often finishing chapters just days before publication.

The 1860s were a turbulent decade in Russia. Tsar Alexander II had emancipated the serfs in 1861, but the reforms created as much chaos as hope. A generation of young radicals — the "men of the sixties" — embraced materialism, utilitarianism, and revolutionary politics. Nikolai Chernyshevsky's novel What Is to Be Done? (1863) became their bible, preaching rational egoism as the path to utopia.

Dostoevsky saw this ideology as spiritually catastrophic. Raskolnikov's "extraordinary man" theory is a direct attack on utilitarian ethics — the belief that murder can be justified by rational calculation of greater goods. The novel asks: what happens to a human soul that tries to live by pure reason alone?

Major Characters

Raskolnikov

Родион Романович Раскольников

The protagonist. A brilliant but impoverished ex-student whose theory of the "extraordinary man" leads him to murder. His name derives from raskol (schism) — he is a man divided against himself.

Sonya Marmeladova

Софья Семёновна Мармеладова

A prostitute with a pure heart. Her faith and unconditional love offer Raskolnikov the path to redemption. She represents Christian suffering and salvation.

Porfiry Petrovich

Порфирий Петрович

The investigating magistrate. He has no evidence but suspects Raskolnikov immediately. His three interrogations are masterpieces of psychological warfare and dark humor.

Svidrigailov

Аркадий Иванович Свидригайлов

Raskolnikov's dark double — a man who has crossed moral boundaries without guilt. His nihilism leads to suicide, showing where Raskolnikov's path ends without redemption.

Themes

The Limits of Rationalism: Raskolnikov's "extraordinary man" theory is an experiment in pure reason — can morality be transcended through intellectual superiority? The novel's answer is devastating: reason alone cannot sustain human life. The body rebels (fever, hallucinations), the conscience revolts, and the soul demands confession.

Guilt and Confession: The "punishment" of the title is not the eight-year sentence; it is the psychological torment that begins immediately after the murder. Raskolnikov's compulsive returns to the crime scene, his near-confessions to strangers, his inability to use the stolen money — all reveal that punishment is built into the act itself.

Resurrection: The Lazarus story is central. Just as Christ raised Lazarus from the dead, Sonya's love offers Raskolnikov spiritual resurrection. The epilogue's final image — Raskolnikov weeping at Sonya's feet — is the first moment of genuine emotional life in the entire novel.

Why It Matters

Crime and Punishment invented the psychological novel as we know it. Before Dostoevsky, novels depicted characters from the outside — what they did and said. Dostoevsky showed us what it feels like to be inside a mind in crisis: the racing thoughts, the irrational associations, the way guilt distorts perception.

Every literary antihero since — from Camus's Meursault to Nabokov's Humbert Humbert to the protagonists of Breaking Bad — descends from Raskolnikov. The novel also prefigured Freudian psychology by decades: the idea that conscious reasoning masks deeper drives, that confession is therapeutic, that the unconscious demands expression.

Most importantly, the novel remains urgently relevant. In an age of utilitarian algorithms, tech-bro "rationalism," and the persistent fantasy that intelligence exempts one from moral law, Raskolnikov's story is a warning: the extraordinary man theory always ends in blood and madness.

"I did not kill a human being, but a principle! I killed the principle, but I did not overstep the barrier, I remained on this side... I was only capable of killing."
— Part V, Chapter 4
