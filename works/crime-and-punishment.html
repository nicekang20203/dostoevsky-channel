Crime and Punishment

Can a superior man transcend morality? The novel that invented the psychological thriller.

The Theory

Rodion Romanovich Raskolnikov is a former student living in crushing poverty in St. Petersburg. In his squalid garret, he has developed a theory: humanity is divided into ordinary people, who must obey the law, and extraordinary people — Napoleons — who have the right to transgress moral law for the greater good.

To prove himself extraordinary, Raskolnikov plans to murder Alyona Ivanovna, an elderly pawnbroker he considers a "louse" — a parasite feeding on the poor. He will use her money to do good, to lift himself and others out of poverty. The murder, he believes, is morally justified.

Pain and suffering are always inevitable for a large intelligence and a deep heart.

— Crime and Punishment

The Murder

The murder itself is botched, chaotic, horrifying. Raskolnikov kills not only the pawnbroker but also her innocent half-sister Lizaveta, who stumbles upon the scene. He barely escapes, and takes almost nothing of value. The theory falls apart immediately.

What follows is not a detective story but a psychological disintegration. Raskolnikov wanders the streets of Petersburg in a fever, tormented by guilt he cannot admit to himself. The novel's genius is showing us a mind that believes it has transcended morality, while revealing that conscience cannot be escaped.

Sonya

Sonya Marmeladova is a young prostitute who has sold herself to support her alcoholic father's family. She is everything Raskolnikov is not: humble, believing, capable of genuine love. She reads to him the story of Lazarus rising from the dead — and eventually leads him to confession and redemption.

Why It Matters

Crime and Punishment invented the psychological thriller. It pioneered the technique of limited third-person narration that would dominate modern fiction. And it posed questions that the twentieth century would have to answer: What happens when men believe they are beyond good and evil? What is the cost of ideology divorced from compassion?
