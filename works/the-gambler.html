The Gambler

Written in 26 days. The novel that brought Anna into his life.

The Trap

In 1866, Dostoevsky was caught in an impossible situation. He had signed a contract with the publisher Stellovsky agreeing to deliver a new novel by November 1. If he failed, Stellovsky would own the rights to all of Dostoevsky's works for nine years — without paying him a kopeck.

By October, he had written nothing. He was consumed by Crime and Punishment, which was being serialized. Friends urged him to hire a stenographer. On October 4, Anna Grigoryevna Snitkina arrived at his apartment.

I know I shall win. Why do I have such a strange certainty? I have staked everything, everything!

— Alexei, The Gambler

26 Days

They worked together from October 4 to October 30. Dostoevsky would dictate for four to six hours; Anna would transcribe her shorthand into longhand each evening. In twenty-six days, they completed the novel.

But Stellovsky had one more trick. On October 31, his office was closed. No one was available to accept the manuscript. The deadline would pass, and Dostoevsky would lose everything. Anna found a solution: they filed the manuscript at a police station, which timestamped it October 31.

The Novel

The Gambler tells the story of Alexei Ivanovich, a young tutor in the household of a Russian general living abroad. Like Dostoevsky himself, Alexei becomes consumed by roulette. He watches himself spiral into degradation, observing his own destruction with clinical detachment.

The novel is a devastating portrait of addiction — the irrational compulsion that overrides reason, the cycle of hope and despair, the self-destruction that feels like freedom.

The Proposal

On November 8, eight days after completing the novel, Dostoevsky asked Anna to marry him — through a fictional plot device. He described a story about an aging artist who falls in love with a young woman named Anya. What would Anya say? Anna replied: that she would love him her whole life.
