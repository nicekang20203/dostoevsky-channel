Demons

A prophetic novel about revolutionary terrorism. The book that predicted the twentieth century.

The Nechayev Affair

In 1869, a young revolutionary named Sergei Nechayev organized the murder of a student in his own cell who questioned his authority. The killing shocked Russia and became the seed for Demons — Dostoevsky's most explicitly political novel.

The novel follows a group of revolutionaries in a provincial Russian town. They are led by Peter Verkhovensky, a ruthless manipulator modeled on Nechayev, who dreams of destroying society and rebuilding it from scratch.

Starting from unlimited freedom, I arrive at unlimited despotism.

— Shigalyov, Demons

Stavrogin

At the center of the novel is Nikolai Stavrogin, a charismatic aristocrat around whom all other characters orbit. Stavrogin embodies complete moral emptiness — a man so spiritually dead he can neither believe nor disbelieve. He has inspired disciples with contradictory ideologies, each believing they understand him.

Prophecy

The novel eerily predicted twentieth-century totalitarianism. Its vision of revolutionary cells using terror to destabilize society, of ideologues who justify any crime in the name of utopia, would be realized in the Russian Revolution and its aftermath. Lenin himself reportedly found the book disturbingly accurate.
