The Idiot

Can a truly good man survive in a corrupt world? Dostoevsky's attempt to portray a "positively beautiful" human being.

Prince Myshkin

Prince Lev Nikolayevich Myshkin returns to Russia after years in a Swiss sanatorium being treated for epilepsy. He is innocent, compassionate, and utterly without guile — a Christ-like figure entering the corrupt world of Petersburg society.

Dostoevsky set himself an almost impossible task: to create a genuinely good character who would not be boring or sentimental. His model was Christ, but also Don Quixote — the holy fool whose goodness the world cannot accommodate.

Beauty will save the world.

— The Idiot

The Five Minutes

In one of the novel's most powerful passages, Myshkin describes a man who faced execution and was reprieved at the last moment. The passage draws directly from Dostoevsky's own experience at Semyonovsky Square — those five minutes when he believed he was about to die.

Nastasya Filippovna

The novel's other center is Nastasya Filippovna, a beautiful woman who was seduced as a child by her guardian. She oscillates between self-destruction and the hope of redemption that Myshkin offers. Her tragic fate shows that goodness alone cannot save — the world destroys what it cannot understand.
