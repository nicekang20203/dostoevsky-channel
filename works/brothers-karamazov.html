The Brothers Karamazov

Dostoevsky's final masterpiece. The greatest novel ever written.

The Brothers

The dissolute Fyodor Karamazov has three legitimate sons: Dmitri, the passionate sensualist; Ivan, the intellectual atheist; and Alyosha, the gentle novice monk. A fourth, Smerdyakov, is illegitimate — possibly Fyodor's son by a village "holy fool" he may have raped.

When Fyodor is murdered, Dmitri is arrested for the crime. But the question of who killed the father extends far beyond the literal murder. All the brothers wished for their father's death — and so, in some sense, all are guilty.

If God does not exist, everything is permitted.

— The Brothers Karamazov

The Grand Inquisitor

Ivan tells Alyosha a "poem" he has composed: Christ returns to Seville during the Spanish Inquisition. He is arrested by the Grand Inquisitor, who condemns Him for giving humanity the terrible burden of freedom. The Church has "corrected" Christ's work by removing freedom and giving people happiness instead.

Christ's only response is to kiss the Inquisitor on his bloodless lips. It is the most powerful confrontation between faith and reason in all of literature.

Legacy

Dostoevsky died three months after completing the novel. He had planned a sequel following Alyosha into the world. The Brothers Karamazov stands as his final testament — on faith, doubt, freedom, and the mystery of the human soul. Freud called it "the most magnificent novel ever written."
