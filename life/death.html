9February 1881

Death in St. Petersburg

Thirty thousand mourners. A prophet goes to his rest.

The Final Days

In late January 1881, Dostoevsky suffered a pulmonary hemorrhage. He had completed The Brothers Karamazov just months before, and was planning a sequel that would follow Alyosha into the world. He would never write it.

On February 9, 1881, with Anna at his bedside, Fyodor Mikhailovich Dostoevsky died. He was fifty-nine years old. His last words, according to Anna, were a request that she read to him from the Gospel of Matthew.

Do not hold me back... my hour has come.

— Dostoevsky's last words to Anna

The Funeral

The funeral, held on February 12, became a national event. An estimated thirty thousand people followed the coffin through the streets of St. Petersburg to the Alexander Nevsky Monastery. Fifteen choirs sang. Seventy-two deputations carried wreaths.

It was the largest funeral Russia had ever seen for a writer. Students, workers, aristocrats, and peasants walked together. The man who had chronicled the suffering of the poor was mourned by all of Russia.

The Legacy

He left behind Anna, who would spend the next thirty-seven years preserving his legacy. He left behind two surviving children, Lyubov and Fyodor. And he left behind a body of work that would shape the twentieth century — influencing Freud, Nietzsche, Kafka, Camus, and countless others.

The man who had faced a firing squad at twenty-eight, who had suffered epilepsy and gambling addiction and poverty and exile, who had lost children and struggled with faith — this man had become, in the end, Russia's prophet. His grave at the Alexander Nevsky Monastery remains a place of pilgrimage.
