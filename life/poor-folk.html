Poor Folk

A new Gogol has appeared! Overnight fame at twenty-four — and the beginning of the fall.

The 4 AM Knock

In January 1846, Dmitry Grigorovich and Nikolai Nekrasov knocked on Dostoevsky's door at four in the morning. They had been up all night reading his manuscript of Poor Folk. They had to tell him immediately: they had discovered a genius.

They rushed the manuscript to Vissarion Belinsky, Russia's most influential critic. Belinsky read it and declared: "A new Gogol has appeared!" Within weeks, the twenty-four-year-old former engineering student was the talk of St. Petersburg's literary world.

A new Gogol has appeared!

— Vissarion Belinsky, 1846

The Novel

Poor Folk tells the story of Makar Devushkin, an aging copying clerk, and Varvara Dobroselova, a young woman he loves from afar. Written entirely in letters between them, it depicts the lives of Petersburg's poor with unprecedented sympathy and psychological depth.

What made the novel revolutionary was its treatment of the poor as full human beings. Makar is not a type or a symbol but a person with dignity, feelings, and an inner life. He reads Gogol's "The Overcoat" and is offended by it — he recognizes himself in the pathetic clerk Akaky Akakievich, and resents being reduced to an object of pity.

The Fall

Success went to his head. Dostoevsky became vain, pompous, and insufferable. He quarreled with the very people who had championed him. His next work, The Double, was poorly received. By 1847, he was being mocked as a has-been.

Looking for meaning and community, he joined the Petrashevsky Circle — a group of young intellectuals who discussed utopian socialism and criticized the autocracy. It was a decision that would nearly cost him his life.
