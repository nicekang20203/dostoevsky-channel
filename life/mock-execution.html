Mock Execution

Five minutes before death. The rifles raised. Then the drums rolled — and life was given back.

Semyonovsky Square

On December 22, 1849, twenty-one members of the Petrashevsky Circle were led to Semyonovsky Square in St. Petersburg. They had spent eight months in the Peter and Paul Fortress, awaiting trial for subversion. Now they stood in the freezing cold, dressed in white execution robes, listening to their death sentences being read.

Dostoevsky was in the second group of three. He watched as the first three prisoners were tied to posts. The firing squad raised their rifles. A priest offered the cross for a final kiss. The drums began their death roll.

What if I were not to die! What if life were given back to me — what infinity! I would turn every minute into an age.

— Prince Myshkin, The Idiot

The Reprieve

Then a drum roll. A white flag. An aide-de-camp galloped into the square with a message: Tsar Nicholas I, in his infinite mercy, had commuted the sentences. It had all been staged — a calculated act of psychological torture designed by the Tsar himself.

Dostoevsky's sentence was changed to four years of hard labor in Siberia, followed by military service. But the experience of those minutes — believing he would die, watching others prepare to die, then having life suddenly restored — never left him.

The Five Minutes

In The Idiot, Prince Myshkin describes a man who faced execution and was reprieved at the last moment. The passage is clearly autobiographical. Myshkin speaks of the five minutes the condemned man had left — how he calculated two minutes for farewells, two for last thoughts, one for a final look around.

Those five minutes became the foundation of Dostoevsky's later philosophy: every moment is precious, every life sacred, and the taking of life — even by the state — is an abomination. The mock execution made him both a deeper Christian and a more radical humanist.
