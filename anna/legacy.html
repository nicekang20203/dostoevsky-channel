1881— 1918

Anna's Legacy

Thirty-seven years as widow. The woman who ensured Dostoevsky's immortality.

The Publisher

After Dostoevsky's death in 1881, Anna revolutionized Russian publishing. She bypassed traditional publishers entirely, selling his complete works directly by mail order. Her apartment became a distribution center. She published seven editions of his complete works between 1881 and 1906.

I gave him my youth, my labor, my entire life. And I am happy that I was able to do this for the man I loved more than anything in the world.

— Anna Dostoevskaya, Reminiscences

The Archivist

Anna saved everything: manuscripts, notebooks, letters, scraps. She organized the archive chronologically and collected materials from correspondents, prison records, and military service documents. Without her, much of what we know about Dostoevsky would be lost.

In 1906, she established the first Dostoevsky museum room in Moscow's Historical Museum — a revolutionary concept at the time. She also wrote her Reminiscences, an intimate portrait that showed his faults along with his genius.

The End

Anna died on June 9, 1918, in Yalta, aged seventy-one. Revolution and war raged around her. In 1968, fifty years after her death, her remains were exhumed and reburied beside Dostoevsky at Alexander Nevsky Monastery in St. Petersburg. At last, they lie together.

Without Anna, there would have been no Gambler, no archive, no memoirs, no museum. We would know Dostoevsky's novels but not the man. She gave him fourteen years of life and thirty-seven years of death. Both were gifts.
