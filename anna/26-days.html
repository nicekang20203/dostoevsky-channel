The 26-Day Sprint

How a young stenographer saved Dostoevsky from ruin — and became his wife.

The Trap

In 1866, Dostoevsky was caught in an impossible contract. The publisher Stellovsky had advanced him 3,000 rubles with a condition: deliver a new novel by November 1, or lose the rights to all his works for nine years. By October, consumed by Crime and Punishment, he had written nothing for Stellovsky.

I would answer that I love you and will love you all my life.

— Anna Snitkina, November 8, 1866

Anna Arrives

On October 4, Anna Grigoryevna Snitkina — a twenty-year-old stenography student — arrived at Dostoevsky's apartment. She was there on business, sent by her teacher. She found a forty-four-year-old man, haggard, epileptic, in debt, and desperate.

They began work immediately. Dostoevsky dictated four to six hours daily; Anna transcribed her shorthand into longhand each evening. The novel was The Gambler — drawn from his own ruinous addiction to roulette.

The Final Trick

On October 30, they finished. But Stellovsky had one more trick: on October 31, his office was closed. No one was available to accept the manuscript. If Dostoevsky couldn't prove delivery, he would lose everything.

Anna found the solution. She took the manuscript to a police station, which officially recorded its receipt with a timestamp of October 31. The contract was fulfilled. Stellovsky was defeated.

The Proposal

Eight days later, on November 8, Dostoevsky asked Anna to marry him — but he did it through a story. He described a plot about an aging artist who falls in love with a young woman named Anya. "What would Anya answer?" he asked. "She would say that she loves you and will love you all her life," Anna replied. They married in February 1867.
