01

Family & Origins

The roots that shaped a literary giant

М
Father

Mikhail Andreevich Dostoevsky

1787–1839

A retired military surgeon who became a physician at Moscow's Mariinsky Hospital for the Poor. Strict, religious, and prone to dark moods, he was murdered by his own serfs in 1839 — an event that haunted Fyodor forever.

М
Mother

Maria Fyodorovna Nechayeva

1800–1837

A merchant's daughter who brought warmth and culture into the household. She taught Fyodor to read using Bible stories. Her death from tuberculosis when he was fifteen left a void he tried to fill through literature.

Childhood at the Hospital

Born in the Mariinsky Hospital for the Poor, young Fyodor grew up surrounded by suffering, death, and the desperate hopes of the destitute. The hospital's patients — peasants, beggars, and outcasts — would later populate his novels.

Seven Siblings

Fyodor was the second of seven children. His elder brother Mikhail became his closest confidant and later his publishing partner in the journals "Time" and "Epoch." When Mikhail died in 1864, Fyodor inherited his debts — a burden that would drive him to desperate productivity.

The Darovoye Estate

The family's small country estate in Tula province offered summer escapes from Moscow. Here young Fyodor encountered peasant life firsthand — their faith, suffering, and resilience. A peasant named Marey who comforted him during a childhood panic would appear in his late writing as a symbol of the Russian soul.

"

I came from a pious Russian family... As far back as I can remember, I remember the love of my parents.

— Dostoevsky, A Writer's Diary
02

Life & Times

From the execution ground to literary immortality

November 11, 1821

Birth in Moscow

Born in the Mariinsky Hospital for the Poor, where his father worked as a physician. This early exposure to suffering would shape his entire literary vision.

1837

Mother's Death & Engineering School

Maria Dostoevskaya dies of tuberculosis. Father sends Fyodor to the Military Engineering Academy in St. Petersburg, where he reads voraciously — Pushkin, Gogol, Balzac, Hugo — while hating his engineering studies.

1839

Father Murdered

Mikhail Andreevich is killed by his own serfs at Darovoye — possibly beaten to death, though officially recorded as an apoplectic stroke. This patricide would echo through The Brothers Karamazov four decades later.

1846

Poor Folk & Literary Fame

His debut novel Poor Folk is praised by Russia's leading critic Vissarion Belinsky, who proclaims a "new Gogol." Overnight, the 24-year-old becomes a literary sensation in St. Petersburg.

December 22, 1849

Mock Execution

Arrested for participating in the Petrashevsky Circle, he faced a firing squad — only to be pardoned at the last moment. Those minutes before the expected death transformed him forever. Prince Myshkin in The Idiot would describe this exact experience.

1850–1854

Siberian Prison

Four years of hard labor in Omsk. Among murderers and thieves, he discovered the depths of human nature — both its darkness and its capacity for redemption. The only book allowed: the New Testament, which he read and reread until its words became part of him.

1866

Crime and Punishment

His masterpiece emerges from financial desperation — written while gambling away advances and fleeing creditors. In the same year, he hired a young stenographer named Anna Snitkina to help write The Gambler in 26 days. She would become his salvation.

February 9, 1881

Death in St. Petersburg

Thirty thousand people attended his funeral. He left behind The Brothers Karamazov — his final testament on faith, doubt, and the mystery of human freedom. Anna would spend the next 37 years preserving his legacy.

03

Anna Snitkina

The woman who saved Dostoevsky

А Anna Grigoryevna
October 1866

26 Days That Changed Everything

Dostoevsky was trapped. A predatory contract with publisher Stellovsky required him to deliver a new novel by November 1st, or forfeit the rights to ALL his works for nine years. He had written nothing. With less than a month remaining, he hired a 20-year-old stenographer recommended by a friend.

Her name was Anna Grigoryevna Snitkina. She would become the most important person in his life.

4 Oct 1866

First Meeting

Anna arrives at Dostoevsky's chaotic apartment. He is 44, epileptic, addicted to gambling, and drowning in debt. She is calm, organized, and determined.

30 Oct 1866

The Gambler Completed

Working 4-6 hours daily, Dostoevsky dictates while Anna transcribes at record speed. The 160-page novel is finished with one day to spare. Contract fulfilled. Career saved.

8 Nov 1866

The Proposal

Dostoevsky proposes through a story — asking Anna's opinion about a plot where an aging writer falls for a young woman. "I would answer that I love you and will love you all my life," she replies.

15 Feb 1867

Marriage

They marry in St. Petersburg. Anna is 20, Dostoevsky is 45. Soon after, they flee Russia to escape creditors, beginning four years of European wandering.

What Anna Became

Stenographer & Editor

All major works after 1866 were dictated to her. She transcribed The Idiot, Demons, A Raw Youth, and The Brothers Karamazov. Her shorthand allowed Dostoevsky to write faster than ever.

Business Manager

She pioneered self-publishing in Russia, cutting out exploitative publishers. By 1880, Dostoevsky was financially stable for the first time in his life. She managed all contracts, handled creditors, and ended his gambling addiction by 1871.

Wife & Mother

She bore four children (two survived to adulthood), weathered his epileptic seizures, endured his gambling relapses, and provided the emotional stability that allowed him to create his greatest works.

Legacy Guardian

After his death in 1881, she lived 37 more years. She published his complete works, wrote memoirs, collected his letters, and in 1906 established the first Dostoevsky museum room. She died in 1918, having ensured his immortality.

"

I gave him my youth, my labor, my entire life. And I am happy that I was able to do this for the man I loved more than anything in the world.

— Anna Dostoevskaya, Reminiscences

04

Major Works

Novels that changed how we understand the human soul

1866

Crime and Punishment Преступление и наказание 죄와 벌

Raskolnikov, a poverty-stricken ex-student in St. Petersburg, convinces himself that he is an extraordinary man — a Napoleon — who can transgress moral law. He murders an elderly pawnbroker with an axe. What follows is not a detective story but a journey into the murderer's disintegrating psyche, his cat-and-mouse game with the brilliant investigator Porfiry, and his eventual confession through the love of Sonya, a prostitute who reads him the story of Lazarus.

Guilt Redemption Nihilism Psychology

1864

Notes from Underground Записки из подполья 지하로부터의 수기

The angry confession of an unnamed narrator who refuses to be a "piano key" in the Crystal Palace of rational utopianism. Widely considered the first existentialist novel, it attacks the naive belief that humans will choose what's good for them once enlightened. "Man is stupid, phenomenally stupid," the Underground Man declares, but he'd rather have his perverse freedom than be "an organ stop."

Free Will Alienation Irrationality
Read More →
1866

The Gambler Игрок 도박꾼

Written in just 26 days with Anna's help, this semi-autobiographical novella draws directly from Dostoevsky's own ruinous gambling addiction. Alexei, a young tutor, becomes consumed by roulette, watching himself spiral into degradation even as he observes his own destruction with clinical detachment. The novel captures the irrational compulsion that kept Dostoevsky at the tables until Anna saved him.

Addiction Self-destruction
Read More →
1869

The Idiot Идиот 백치

Can a truly good man survive in a corrupt world? Prince Myshkin, a Christ-like figure returning to Russia after treatment for epilepsy in Switzerland, possesses infinite compassion and honesty. He becomes entangled with the beautiful, tormented Nastasya Filippovna and the proud Aglaya. His goodness proves powerless against the darkness around him. Myshkin's famous description of facing execution — those eternal five minutes — came directly from Dostoevsky's mock execution.

Innocence Beauty Tragedy
Read More →
1872

Demons Бесы 악령

A prophetic political novel about revolutionary terrorism, inspired by the real-life murder of a student by the nihilist cell led by Sergei Nechayev. Stavrogin, the charismatic center around which all characters orbit, embodies complete moral emptiness — a man so spiritually dead he can neither believe nor disbelieve. The novel eerily predicted 20th-century totalitarianism.

Nihilism Revolution Evil
Read More →
1880

The Brothers Karamazov Братья Карамазовы 카라마조프가의 형제들

Dostoevsky's final and greatest novel. The murder of the dissolute Fyodor Karamazov sets his three sons against each other: the intellectual atheist Ivan, the sensual Dmitri, and the saintly Alyosha. Ivan's "Grand Inquisitor" chapter — where Christ returns to Seville only to be condemned by the Church — remains the most powerful confrontation between faith and reason in literature. Contains echoes of his father's murder.

Faith Doubt Freedom Patricide
